    Flight left at 6:20 AM.
  • Dec. 21, 1988
    Pan American flight 103, a Boeing 747, took off from London, bound for New York City. It exploded over the town of Locerkbie; 270 people from 21 countries were killed, including all 259 passengers and crewmembers plus 11 people on the ground in Lockerbie.
  • Dec. 28, 1988
    British investigators concluded that a bomb in the luggage compartment caused the explosion.
  • Dec. 29, 1988
    As a result of the Pan Am terrorist attach, The Federal Aviation Administration imposed new regulations on American Airlines that fly out of Pan Am airports in Western Europe and the Middle East.
  • Dec. 29, 1988
    The Air Accidents Investigation Branch of the British Department of Transport determined that residue on the wreckage was consistent with "high-performance plastic explosive."
  • Jan. 1, 1989
    Accompanied by British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher, 700 mourners, attended a memorial at Dryfesdale Church in Lockerbiefor. About 200 relatives of the victims and 100 Pan Am World Employees were in attendance.
  • Jan. 18, 1989
    At an interfaith memorial was held at the Carrier Dome, SU Chancellor Melvin A. Eggers announced a Rememberance Scholarship fund. Approximately 15,000 people in attendance. The Daily Orange - the student-run newspaper at SU - released a special edition dedicated to the 35 student victims, and the SU drama department performed a special tribute.
  • Jan. 21, 1989
    SU ROTC held its own memorial for Timothy Michael Cardwell, an Army ROTC Scholarship winner, a volunteer in the ROTC Ranger program and a sergeant in the 403rd Army Reserve National Guard at Syracuse. Almost 60 ROTC members, along with the Hendricks Chapel Choir, attended the service.
  • Jan. 31, 1989
    A single casket was buried in the Dryfesdale Cemetery to represent the 17 victims whose bodies were not found during the searches following the crash.
  • Feb. 3, 1989
    Inaccurate reports said western intelligence came to the conclusion that the bombing was commissioned by Iran and carried out by with support from Syria and Libya.
  • March 16, 1989
    SU Chancellor Melvin A. Eggers visited Lockerbie. While there, he placed a wreath at the Grave of Missing Persons.
  • April 3, 1989
    Relatives and friends of the victims hold a demonstration and memorial vigil in Washington, D.C.
  • Feb. 13, 1991
    Three years after the bombing, the Scottish Fatal Accident Inquiry was completed and determined the attack to be a "criminal act of murder."
  • Nov. 14, 1991
    The United States indicted two Libyan intelligence agents - Abdelbaset Ali Mohmed Al Megrahi and Al Amin Khalifa Fhimah - and left open the possibility of military retaliation for the Pan Am bombing.
  • Nov. 30, 1991DELETE
    The Scottish police found further evidence in Sweden suggesting that a Pelestinian known as Abu Talb helped carry out the bomob plot. The police examined Talb's personal belongings in his apartment in Uppsala, Sweden, and discovered clothing similar to burned shreds of cloth found in the wreckage.
  • March, 23, 1995
    The FBI announces a $4 million award for the two suspects.
  • April, 5, 1999
    More than ten years after the Pan Am Flight, Fhimah and Al Megrahi - the two Libyans charged with planting the bomb - arrived in the Netherlands to face trial for the crime.
  • May 3, 2000
    The trial started more than 11 years after the tragedy. It opened at the United States Air Force base Camp Zeist in the Netherlands with a specially convened Scottish court. Fhimah and Al Megrahi pleaded not guilty to murdering the 270 people on the Pan Am flight.
  • June 16, 2000
    The owner of MEBO AG, a Swiss firm that made the timer allegedly used for the bomb, identifed Al Megrahi, saying they did business together.
  • July 11, 2000DELETE
    A garment store owner identifies one of the accused; he said to have flown to Malta on the day of the blast.
  • August, 22 2000
    The defense attorneys sought classifed documents from the CIA for cross examination of the witnesses.
  • Jan. 10, 2001
    The prosecution closed its case, saying they did not know how the bomb got onto the aircraft.
  • Jan. 31, 2001
    Three Scottish Judges found Fhimah not guilty and Al Megrahi guilty of 270 counts of murder in a court in the Netherlands.
  • Feb. 7, 2001.
    Al Megrahi lodged an appeal against his conviction.
  • March 14, 2002.
    Al Megrahi lost the appeal because the prosecution's circumstantial evidence was sufficient to uphold the conviction.
  • August 20, 2009.
    Al Megrahi was released on compassionate grounds claiming he probably had only three months to live because of prostate cancer. He was freed from Scottish jail, but stirred controversy when he outlived his three months.
  • Jan. 31, 2001.DELETE
    Al Megrahi was convicted of 270 counts of murder by a panel of three Scottish judges presiding in a special court in the Netherlands to which Col. Muammar el-Qaddafi had allowed his transfer.
  • May 20, 2012.
    Megrahi died at the age of 60.
